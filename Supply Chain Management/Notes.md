## 1.1 What is Operations and Supply Chain Management

Historically: Companies simply took in inputs and produced outputs \
Now: Companies will look at where other companies are getting their inputs from (upstream) and where their outputs are going (downstream).

### 1.1.1 What is Operations and Supply Chain Management?

Operations and supply chain managers focus on how to **develop capabilities to design, produce and deliver** products and services in a competitive market.

**Note**: Supply chain management deals with *goods and services*. Not purely about physical products.

#### "Develop capabilities" What kind of capabilities?
* New products & service features
* Decrease costs to deliver or produce goods and services
* Improve quality, quality of product/quality of production process/quality of delivering the product
* Reduce time to market
* Ability to innovate

## 1.5 Responsiveness vs Efficiency
### 1.5.1 Classic Supply Chain Models
1. Responsive Supply Chain
2. Efficient Supply Chain

Learn to compare and contrast these 2 models. How do they fit in the strategic objectives for a company.

### 1.5.2 Supply Chain Operating Model
#### Two ends of a spectrum of a supply chain operating model:
1. Efficient supply chain is *cost-focused*
    * Reducing costs
    * Better utilization of assets
    * More efficient, productive
    * Better yield capacity
2. Responsive supply chain is *speed-focused*
    * Faster response to changes in customer delivery requirements (eg. volume or lead time)
    * Quickly get products to customer

#### How the different supply chain models affect the company's overall strategy
||Efficient SC (Cost Focus)|Responsive SC (Speed Focus)|
|---|---|---|
|SC Operating Model|Supply the predicted demand efficiently at the lowest possible cost|Respond quickly to unpredictable demand in order to minimize OOS, lost sales, clearance, obsolete inventory|
|Capacity Strategy|Maintain high average utilization rate|Excess buffer capacity for flexibility to react to demand|
|Inventory Strategy|High turnover and minimize inventory stocks throughout the supply chain|Significant/extra stock of parts or finished goods for buffer to respond to surges in demand|
|Lead Time Strategy|Reduce lead time as long as it doesn't increase costs|Invest aggressively in ways too reduce lead time|
|Supplier Capability|Select suppliers with better cost and quality|Select suppliers with better speed, flexibility, responsiveness|
|Logistics Strategy|Greater reliance on low cost modes (eg. ship)|Greater reliance of fast modes of transport (eg. by air)|

* Capacity: rate at which the company can product goods/services, how many people you can serve
* Utilization: percentage of how much capacity you are using
* Lead time: Time between when a customer places an order to when they recieve it.
* Logistics: Transport of stock

### 1.5.3 Why Different Models?
What might lead a company to be more cost focused, or more speed focused?
* Mainly depends on the type of product/service that you are selling/providing

#### If the type of product is more *functional products*, there is...
* Relatively stable environment
* Lower demand uncertainty
* More predictable/stable demand
* Longer product life
* Lower inventory cost
* Lower profit margins
* Lower product variety & higher volume
* Lower OOS and obsolescence cost - costs you less if customer wants the product and you don't have it

#### If the type of product is more *innovative products*, there is...
* Relatively unpredictable environment
* Higher demand uncertainty
* More unpredictable demand/difficulty in forecasting demand
* Shorter product life
* Higher inventory cost
* Higher profit margins
* Higher product variety and lower volume
* Higher OOS and obsolescence cost - costs you more if customer wants the product and you don't have it

*Functional* products and *innovative* products need different supply chains.

*Functional* products need Effcient SC
*Innovative* products need Responsive SC

### 1.5.4 How strategic (long term) decisions drive tactical (medium term) and operational (short term) decisions

#### Example of a retail company
*Strategic* decision...
  * Efficient and low cost supply chain \
*Tactically*, this means...
  * Low operating costs
  * High asset utilization/ROA \
*Operationally*, this means...
  * VMI - vendor managed inventory
  * DSD - direct store delivery (bypassing warehouses)
  * Full truckloads - delivering full truckloads instead of half a truck, make the most use out of transport

### 1.5.5 Summary
* Ultimate goal is to match supply of a product with the demand of that product
* Ideal supply chain model varies depending on the type of product
* Mixing both supply chains: agile supply chain

## 1.6 Network Configuration

Some components of a supply chain:
- Warehouses
- Suppliers
- Customers
- Markets

These components can be global!

How to configure the supply chain to meet different markets? Where to have warehouses and facilities?

### 1.6.1 Types of network configurations
1. Centralized networks
2. Decentralized networks

#### Example of a global supply chain network
* Example: you have markets in multiple regions
* Where should you put the warehouses to supply those markets
* Where should you manufacture
* Where should your suppliers be
* Where should you have all of these located with respect to your customers

Solution #1:
- Suppliers, manufacturers, warehouses in 1 region
- From this 1 region, distribute globally
- Centralized sourcing, manufacturing, distribution

Solution #2:
- Manufacture in 1 region
- Suppliers, warehouses in all key regions
- Suppliers, warehouses from any region can support demand in other regions
- Centralized manufacturing
- Global distribution

Solution #3:
- Every region has its own suppliers, manufacturing, distribution
- Decentralized sourcing, manufacturing, distribution

Solution #4:
- Global sourcing, manufacturing
- Decentralized distribution

Most to Least centralized:
1. Solution #1
2. Solution #2
3. Solution #4
4. Solution #3

### 1.6.2 Advantages and Disadvantages 
#### ...Of A Centralized Network
#### Advantages
* Less inventory - you have less warehouses (eg. 1 warehouse in 1 region vs 3 warehouses in 3 regions)
* Higher pooling effect = Less need to cater to different demand from different regions = Less need for safety stock
* Higher availability of inventory
* Higher shipping volume = Easier to negotiate for higher discounts, better deals for shipping/3PL
* Less complex, easier to coordinate
* Easier to standardize practices
* Higher economics of scale (improvements in manufacturing will affect 100% of your inventory instead of 33% in 1 affected region) = lower cost
#### Disadvantages
* Higher transportation cost and timing (further distance to transport to other regions)
* Decreased responsiveness

#### ...Of A Decentralized Network
Basically the opposite of centralized network
#### Advantages
* Lower transportation cost and timing (less distance to transport)
* Increased responsiveness
#### Disadvantages
* More total inventory
* Less pooling effect = More need for safety stock
* Lower availability
* Lower shipment volume = Harder to negotiate with shipping
* More complex, harder to coordinate
* Harder to standardize practices
* Less economics of scale

### 1.6.3 When to choose a centralized or decentralized netowrk?
||Centralized Strategy|Decentralized Strategy|
|---|---|---|
|Demand Environment|Stable/predictable|Uncertain\Unpredictable|
|Velocity|Lower|Higher|
|Availability|Higher|Lower|
|Points of sale (delivery points)|Few|Many|
|Distribution cost per weight (Cost of distribution)|Lower|Higher|
|Responsiveness|Lower|Higher|
|Delivery lead times|Longer|Shorter|
|Delivery customization|Less|More|
|Supply Chain|Efficient supply chain|Responsive supply chain|

### 1.6.4 Summary
* Centralized network: Efficient supply chain, cost focused operating model
* Decentralized network: Responsive supply chain, speed focused operating model
